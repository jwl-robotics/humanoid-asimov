While walking its loop, the robot estimates its own camera-mount rotation online — no calibration rig, only the onboard sensors the VIO already uses. It also carries an FD-verified camera–IMU time-offset state (td), held frozen in the headline demo while the mount calibrates online. Validation: inject a known mount error and recover it. The follow-up below revisits both limits with active calibration: a gentle neck scan, and td unfrozen against a known injected clock offset.

The idea

A real robot's head camera is never mounted exactly where the CAD says, and its frame timestamps are a few ms out of sync with the IMU clock. The classic fix is a calibration rig (wave a checkerboard before deploying). In-situ self-calibration instead has the robot recover those errors itself, while just walking — robust to lifetime drift, no downtime.

The Stage-2 VIO filter is augmented with a camera-mount rotation extrinsic δφ_HC (3) and a time offset td (1); the error state grows 21 → 25, with the calibration states placed before the stochastic clone so cloning stays a clean truncate-then-append. Both enter the existing relative-rotation vision measurement, so no new sensor is needed.

The observability structure (the real lesson)

A pure yaw-loop observes only the mount component perpendicular to the turn axis. The component along the yaw axis is nearly invisible per frame (only the gait's ripple excites it), and the time offset is weakly observable too — the loop sits near the constant-angular-rate degeneracy where a common time shift cancels. This isn't a bug: the finite-difference check passes to ~1e-7, and the filter's covariance reports the anisotropy (the φ_y funnel stays ~6× wider than φ_x).

TakeawayA consistent filter recovers what the trajectory makes observable and honestly flags what it cannot — a stronger result than a lucky full recovery. Full 3-axis + time-offset calibration needs richer rotational excitation than a single yaw-loop — which is exactly what the next section goes and buys.
Active calibration — the neck-scan retry

If the yaw-loop cannot excite the weak axes, move the camera: walk.py has a neck-pitch “look-around” scan. This was tried during Stage 3 and hurt — the then-~6 mrad front-end lost KLT tracks under camera pitch. With the front-end sharpened to an honest ~2 mrad (IRLS refit), run_neckscan.py retries it properly: inject the same 3.1° mount error plus a 10 ms camera clock offset, estimate both (td unfrozen), and sweep gentle (amplitude, frequency) scans — measuring the observability gain and the track-quality cost, three seeds on the key points.

Three honest findings. (1) The old failure mode is gone: front-end success stays 74–77% at every amplitude and rate tried — the sharpened front-end holds its tracks under pitch, so the Stage-3-era “scanning hurts” conclusion was a front-end artifact, not physics. The cost has moved downstream: past ~0.3 Hz the fusion gate starts rejecting (44→66% acceptance at gentle scans, collapsing to ~40% at 0.6 Hz, with seed-noisy estimates to match — the φ_y median of 2.7 there scatters 0.2–4.9 across seeds). (2) The scan pays on the mount axes: the weak-axis funnel halves (7.5→4.2 mrad), the recovered error halves with it (15.5→7.3 mrad), and φ_z is pinned to ~0.1 mrad. Weak-axis errors sit near 2σ in both cases — mildly optimistic on the hardest axis, unchanged by the scan. (3) A surprise on td: the injected 10 ms clock offset is recovered to +0.6 ms without any scan — the walking gait’s angular-rate ripple already excites it; the constant-rate degeneracy argument applies to the smoothed loop, not to the gait riding on it. Freezing td in the headline demo was conservative, not necessary.

TakeawayActive calibration works once the front-end is good enough to survive it: a moderate look-around (~5° at 0.3 Hz) buys a 2× tighter weak-axis funnel and a 2× better mount estimate at zero front-end cost — and the chi-square gate acceptance rate is the online signal that tells you when you are scanning too hard.
