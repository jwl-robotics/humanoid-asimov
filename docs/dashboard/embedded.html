▍ Stage 4 — The Sim-to-Real Gap

embedded pipelinetiming > amplitudeablation study

The sim-to-real gap for a low-cost humanoid is dominated by the embedded pipeline — finite sensor resolution, sequential CAN reads, transport latency, sample jitter — not physics fidelity. This stage injects those effects and stress-tests the estimator to find which ones actually matter.

The model

apply_embedded_noise corrupts a logged dataset by re-sampling each sensor stream at its true (perturbed) capture time and quantizing: encoder/IMU quantization (14-bit / 16-bit LSBs), per-joint CAN stagger (the bus reads joints sequentially), transport latency, and sample-interval jitter. Physics is untouched — only the measurement pipeline is made realistic.

The finding: timing dominates, amplitude is negligible
Left: ESKF drift under each effect alone. Amplitude effects (quantization, jitter) are no worse than clean; timing effects (inter-joint stagger, latency) blow drift up several-fold. Right: drift climbs steeply with latency.

Why it ties back to Stage 3

The dominant killer is timing — latency and the relative skew between sensor streams — while sensor precision barely registers — to close the sim-to-real gap you model the pipeline's timing structure, not its amplitude.

TakeawayLatency is the dominant drift driver — and Stage 3’s camera–IMU time-offset state (td) is the template for the defense: model per-stream time offsets as states and calibrate them online — given a trajectory that excites them — instead of trusting timestamps. Stage 4 says why; Stage 3 shows how (and where the excitation has to come from).
