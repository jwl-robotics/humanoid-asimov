▍ Stage 5 — Closed-Loop Navigation

estimator → planner → command4/4 waypointson the estimate5b: 3-circuit showdown

The capstone slice: the robot walks to a sequence of waypoints using only its own onboard state estimate, closing the loop sensors → estimator → waypoint planner → VelocityCommand → gait steering — the same command interface the real control stack accepts. The follow-up showdown below (5b) then stresses this loop the way the earlier stages predict it breaks: three circuits, VIO fused inside the loop, and injected transport latency — naive vs delay-compensated.

The loop

Every control step the ESKF ingests the onboard sensors and reports a pose; a WaypointNavigator turns that pose into a body-frame VelocityCommand(vx, vy, vyaw) toward the active waypoint, which steers the walking gait. Because the planner sees only the estimate, estimation error surfaces directly as navigation error — which is exactly why an accurate estimator matters for navigation.

The through-line

The estimated and true paths diverge because heading is unobservable from IMU + contact alone — the same limitation Stage 1 diagnosed and Stage 2's VIO fixes. Navigation makes the consequence concrete and visible: a few degrees of yaw drift becomes tens of centimeters of position error at the goal.

Continuous 3-lap drift with a realistic uncalibrated IMU bias: leg-odometry and the ESKF accumulate unbounded yaw drift (17° / 7°), while VIO stays bounded at ~1° across all three laps as vision keeps pinning the yaw-axis bias. Position error (right) oscillates over the closed loop.

And the VIO-over-ESKF win is robust, not a lucky seed: across 5 seeds VIO beats the ESKF on drift and gyro-z bias on every one (median 0.15% vs 0.49%; bg,z 0.19 vs 1.66 mrad/s).

TakeawayThe whole project reads as one arc — leg-odometry and IMU drift in yaw, VIO anchors it, self-calibration keeps the sensors honest, the embedded-noise study says timing is what breaks on hardware, and navigation closes the loop and shows why every earlier stage mattered.
Stage 5b — the closed-loop showdown (3 circuits)

The follow-up stresses the loop the way the earlier stages predict it breaks: the same 3-circuit waypoint course (12 waypoints, ~45 s, ~38 m) driven by four estimator configurations, each navigating on its own estimate — the clean-timing ESKF; VIO in the loop (head-cam frames rendered inside the control loop at the 2 Hz keyframe cadence and fused as in Stage 2); and 5 ms transport latency + 2 ms CAN stagger applied online, fused naively vs through the Stage-4b buffer-and-replay filter steering on its now-cast. Same seed throughout.

Metric honesty first: the headline numbers are the true closest approach to each waypoint and the estimate-vs-truth gap over time. Final-position error is deliberately not reported as a headline — the course returns home, so closing geometry flatters it. And a closed navigation loop hides estimator damage by construction: the planner continuously re-aims at the goal as it believes it to be, so waypoint success stays high while the truth quietly walks away — the gap and yaw columns are where the damage shows.

Three honest findings. (1) The decisive variable was not on the matrix. A baseline check (data/nav5b_eskf005_check.npz) ran the course with run_nav.py's short-path contact trust σc = 0.05: over three circuits of repeated 90° pivots the Stage-2 turn-slip bg runaway accumulates and the run fails — 10/12 waypoints, a 6.9 m peak gap, 98° of terminal yaw error. In-loop VIO rescues that mistuned filter (12/12 at 17°), and simply loosening to σc = 0.15 — the Stage-2 lesson — fixes it outright; the matrix above runs 0.15 everywhere. (2) 5 ms of latency is survivable in closed loop — but it is not free: the naive run finishes the course, yet carries ~54% more terminal yaw error and the widest gap excursions; replay compensation returns every column to the clean baseline, consistent with the open-loop Stage-4b result. (3) In-loop VIO adds little on this course: at the 2 Hz keyframe cadence the sharp pivots break KLT tracks (37 measurements, 28 fused, in 151 keyframes — even with a gyro-seeded LK initial guess), and 44 s at σc = 0.15 accrues only ~3.6° of yaw for it to fix. Vision earns its keep on long horizons (the 3-lap monitor above) and as the rescue in finding 1 — not on a well-tuned short course.

TakeawayClosed-loop navigation grades on a curve — re-aiming hides estimator error, so success metrics must be chosen adversarially (true closest approach, est-vs-truth gap, terminal yaw). On those metrics the stack holds: replay compensation neutralizes injected latency in the loop, and the one configuration that truly fails is the one that over-trusts its contact model — the same lesson Stage 2 taught, now with a navigation-grade price tag.
